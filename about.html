Mission & Vision

What We Stand For

Vision

"A world in which digital transformations of economies, societies, and politics are governed by legitimate, inclusive frameworks shaped by rigorous, independent knowledge."

Embedded within the Global Governance Centre at the Geneva Graduate Institute, the Global Digital Governance Lab is uniquely positioned to bridge scholarship and international policymaking, and to serve as a platform for global conversations on digital governance.

The Lab produces independent, social science-based rigorous research on the digital economies, infrastructures, rights, and technologies reshaping political and social life. Drawing on Geneva's singular position as a hub of multilateral diplomacy and international organisation, it convenes researchers, policymakers, industry actors, and civil society to connect knowledge and practice.

The Lab brings these key actors together to help them understand what current digital governance processes include, what they exclude, and how they can be improved. We bring the analytical tools of the social sciences to bear on what those processes make possible, what they foreclose, and how they can be made to work better for the benefit of all.

"Few institutions approach digital governance debates from the perspective of global governance — the institutions, norms, and processes through which the international community negotiates collective rules."

Context

The Challenge & The Gap

Understanding why an independent, Geneva-based digital governance lab is needed now.

The Challenge

The governance of digital technologies has become one of the defining challenges of international order. Digital technologies, infrastructure, and data standards are reshaping economies, rights, and political relations faster than the frameworks meant to govern them can adapt.

Geopolitical competition is increasingly mediated through digital infrastructure, platforms, and standards, fragmenting what was once imagined as a unified global digital space into competing regulatory spheres and technological blocs. The costs of that fragmentation fall disproportionately on those with least voice in the decisions driving it.

The Gap

Current digital governance debates are fragmented, highly technical, and dominated by a small number of powerful states, companies, and regulatory blocs. Many of the actors most affected by digital transformation — particularly from the Global South — remain underrepresented in the venues where the rules are set.

While many research centres develop digital technologies, and others engage with domestic regulatory questions, few approach these debates from the perspective of global governance. Fewer still do so as independent social science research in close proximity to the multilateral system.

The Opportunity

Why Geneva.
Why Now.

Geneva is one of the cities where the rules of global digital governance are being negotiated. The ITU, UNCTAD, UNDP, the Human Rights Council, the Geneva liaison of the UN Office for Digital and Emerging Technologies, and a dense network of standards bodies and diplomatic missions all sit within walking distance of one another.

The WSIS+20 review and the implementation of the Global Digital Compact will reshape the multilateral digital order over the coming years, and Geneva is one of the places where that work is being done.

Independent, rigorous, and inclusive research undertaken now can shape the frameworks that will govern digital transformation for decades to come.

WSIS+20 Review Process
The World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS) +20 review is a once-in-a-decade opportunity to reshape the multilateral frameworks governing digital technologies. Geneva hosts many of the key negotiations.
Global Digital Compact
The GDC, adopted at the UN Summit of the Future in September 2024, establishes new frameworks for data governance, AI, and digital cooperation. Its implementation involves ongoing negotiations centred in Geneva.
UN Office for Digital & Emerging Technologies
The newly established UN Office for Digital and Emerging Technologies has a Geneva liaison, placing it at the heart of the multilateral digital governance ecosystem where the Lab operates.
AI Governance Frameworks
Multiple simultaneous negotiations — at the ITU, within UNCTAD, and through ad hoc processes — are producing AI governance frameworks. The Lab is positioned to offer independent analysis and convene key actors in these debates.
Why the Graduate Institute
The Geneva Graduate Institute combines academic credibility, methodological independence, and a multidisciplinary faculty already producing leading research on digital sovereignty, infrastructure, and rights. Its track record hosting the Global Health Centre — a reference point in its field — provides a model for the GDG Lab.
